**Author:** Leo Linbeck III, Stanford Graduate School of Business
**Reviewer:** Literature Review Agent
**Date:** 2026-03-27

## 1. Missing Key Literature (22 Specific Citations with Justification)

The paper's 28 references are heavily weighted toward foundational philosophical texts and a small number of AI governance documents. The following works represent significant omissions that would strengthen the paper's scholarly positioning.

### Virtue Ethics and AI Safety

1. **Floridi, Luciano & Cowls, Josh. "A Unified Framework of Five Principles for AI in Society." *Harvard Data Science Review* 1, no. 1 (2019).**
   - *Justification:* Floridi is the most-cited living philosopher of technology. His principled framework (beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice, explicability) is the dominant alternative to the virtue-ethics approach the paper advocates. Engaging with Floridi is essential for positioning the conscience faculty against the mainstream.

2. **Wallach, Wendell & Allen, Colin. *Moral Machines: Teaching Robots Right from Wrong.* Oxford University Press, 2009.**
   - *Justification:* The foundational text on machine morality distinguishes top-down and bottom-up approaches to embedding ethics in autonomous systems -- the exact dichotomy the paper claims to transcend. Not citing Wallach leaves a significant gap in the machine ethics lineage.

3. **Coeckelbergh, Mark. *AI Ethics.* MIT Press, 2020.**
   - *Justification:* Coeckelbergh's relational approach to AI ethics provides a direct counterpoint to the paper's individualist/subsidiarity framing. His argument that moral agency is constituted relationally (not individually) challenges the paper's Newman-derived primacy of individual conscience.

4. **Coeckelbergh, Mark. "Artificial Intelligence, Responsibility Attribution, and a Relational Justification of Explainability." *Science and Engineering Ethics* 26 (2020): 2051-2068.**
   - *Justification:* Directly addresses how moral responsibility distributes across human-AI systems -- a question the paper's principal-sovereignty model must confront.

5. **Moor, James H. "The Nature, Importance, and Difficulty of Machine Ethics." *IEEE Intelligent Systems* 21, no. 4 (2006): 18-21.**
   - *Justification:* Establishes the canonical taxonomy of ethical agents (ethical impact agents, implicit ethical agents, explicit ethical agents, full ethical agents). The conscience faculty implicitly claims to produce explicit ethical agents; this classification should be engaged.

6. **Taddeo, Mariarosaria & Floridi, Luciano. "How AI Can Be a Force for Good." *Science* 361, no. 6404 (2018): 751-752.**
   - *Justification:* The paper's claim that centralized governance is structurally flawed needs to engage with Floridi and Taddeo's counterargument that distributed AI ethics requires coordinated institutional support.

### Decentralized AI Governance

7. **Werbach, Kevin. *The Blockchain and the New Architecture of Trust.* MIT Press, 2018.**
   - *Justification:* The most developed scholarly treatment of how decentralized trust architectures function. The paper's trust-score mechanism for multi-agent interaction (Section 5.2) operates in the same conceptual space but does not reference this literature.

8. **De Filippi, Primavera & Wright, Aaron. *Blockchain and the Law: The Rule of Code.* Harvard University Press, 2018.**
   - *Justification:* Analyzes the tension between decentralized governance and legal accountability -- precisely the tension the paper's subsidiarity model must resolve when agents cause harm.

9. **Rahwan, Iyad. "Society-in-the-Loop: Programming the Algorithmic Social Contract." *Ethics and Information Technology* 20 (2018): 5-14.**
   - *Justification:* Proposes a society-in-the-loop framework that occupies the middle ground between the paper's bottom-up conscience and top-down regulation. This is the most developed alternative to both extremes.

10. **Dafoe, Allan. "AI Governance: A Research Agenda." Centre for the Governance of AI, Future of Humanity Institute (2018).**
    - *Justification:* The standard research agenda for AI governance. The paper's claim to offer a paradigm alternative to centralized governance needs to engage with this framing.

### Ostrom, Commons, and Digital Governance

11. **Hess, Charlotte & Ostrom, Elinor. *Understanding Knowledge as a Commons.* MIT Press, 2007.**
    - *Justification:* Ostrom's own extension of commons theory to information/knowledge goods -- far more relevant to digital AI commons than the fisheries/forests work the paper cites.

12. **Frischmann, Brett, Madison, Michael & Strandburg, Katherine. *Governing Knowledge Commons.* Oxford University Press, 2014.**
    - *Justification:* The leading empirical study of knowledge commons governance. Provides both supporting evidence and important qualifications for applying Ostrom to digital domains.

13. **Benkler, Yochai. *The Wealth of Networks.* Yale University Press, 2006.**
    - *Justification:* The foundational analysis of peer production and commons-based governance in networked environments. The paper's communal feedback mechanism is a form of commons-based peer production that should be situated in this literature.

### Dual-Process Theory Critiques

14. **Gigerenzer, Gerd. "On the Supposed Evidence for Libertarian Paternalism." *Review of Philosophy and Psychology* 6 (2015): 361-383.**
    - *Justification:* Gigerenzer's critique that Kahneman's System 1/System 2 dichotomy is overly simplistic and that "fast" heuristics are often ecologically rational. The paper builds its entire computational architecture on the dual-process framework without acknowledging this major critique.

15. **Evans, Jonathan St B.T. & Stanovich, Keith E. "Dual-Process Theories of Higher Cognition: Advancing the Debate." *Perspectives on Psychological Science* 8, no. 3 (2013): 223-241.**
    - *Justification:* The most cited revision of dual-process theory, which significantly qualifies the clean System 1/System 2 distinction the paper relies on. Evans and Stanovich argue for a more nuanced "Type 1/Type 2" framing.

16. **Mercier, Hugo & Sperber, Dan. *The Enigma of Reason.* Harvard University Press, 2017.**
    - *Justification:* Proposes that reasoning evolved for argumentation, not truth-seeking -- a view that would complicate the paper's assumption that System 2 deliberation produces better moral judgments.

### Agentic AI Safety

17. **Shavit, Yonadav et al. "Practices for Governing Agentic AI Systems." OpenAI White Paper (2023).**
    - *Justification:* The most detailed industry framework for agentic AI safety. The paper critiques centralized approaches but does not engage with this specific proposal, which includes tool-use monitoring and human-in-the-loop mechanisms similar to the conscience faculty.

18. **Chan, Alan et al. "Harms from Increasingly Agentic Algorithmic Systems." *FAccT '23* (2023).**
    - *Justification:* Provides the most systematic taxonomy of harms from agentic AI systems. The paper's Section 2 critique would be strengthened by engaging with this empirical framework.

19. **Anthropic. "Challenges in Evaluating AI Systems." (2023) and related work on Constitutional AI scaling.**
    - *Justification:* The paper cites Amodei (2022) on Constitutional AI but does not engage with subsequent developments in scalable oversight and evaluation -- the exact technical problems the conscience faculty claims to solve.

### Newman Scholarship

20. **Gage, Logan & Aquino, Frederick. "Newman's Illative Sense Re-Examined." *PhilArchive* (2025).**
    - *Justification:* The most recent analytic re-evaluation of the illative sense, proposing three formal interpretations that could strengthen or complicate the paper's computational mapping.

21. **Nichols, Aidan. "John Henry Newman and the Illative Sense: A Re-consideration." *Scottish Journal of Theology* 38 (1985): 347-368.**
    - *Justification:* Raises the individualism objection to Newman's illative sense -- that it privileges subjective judgment over communal verification. This is directly relevant to Section 6.1's anthropomorphism objection and the broader question of whether individual conscience can be reliably implemented.

22. **Ker, Ian. *Newman on Vatican II.* Oxford University Press, 2014.**
    - *Justification:* Provides important context on how Newman's primacy of conscience was received by institutional Catholicism -- relevant given the paper's reliance on the Letter to the Duke of Norfolk and Catholic social teaching.

## 2. Coverage Assessment by Framework

### Framework 1: Aristotelian-Thomistic Ethics
**Current coverage:** Aquinas (Summa Theologiae), Aristotle (Nicomachean Ethics), MacIntyre (After Virtue), Vallor (Technology and the Virtues), Hagendorff (virtue-based AI ethics)
**Rating:** MODERATE
**Assessment:** The philosophical core is well-cited with primary sources. However, the paper does not engage with the broader machine ethics literature that has already attempted to operationalize virtue ethics in AI systems. Wallach & Allen (2009) is a critical omission -- they established the top-down vs. bottom-up distinction the paper builds on without attribution. Moor's taxonomy of ethical agents is also missing. The Thomistic scholarship is limited to primary sources; secondary scholarship (e.g., Finnis, Haldane, McInerny on practical reason) would strengthen the philosophical argument. Vallor is cited but her specific critiques of technological solutionism are not engaged.

**Key gap:** No engagement with the existing machine ethics literature that has attempted similar virtue-based implementations.

### Framework 2: Newman's Epistemology
**Current coverage:** Newman (Grammar of Assent, Letter to the Duke of Norfolk), Ratzinger (On Conscience)
**Rating:** WEAK
**Assessment:** The paper relies entirely on primary Newman texts and one sympathetic interpreter (Ratzinger). There is no engagement with the substantial secondary literature on the illative sense, including analytic re-evaluations (Gage & Aquino 2025), critiques of Newman's individualism (Nichols 1985), or comparisons with other epistemological traditions (Martin 2015 on Newman and Reid). The computational mapping of the illative sense to the Clarity x Stakes scoring system is the paper's most original contribution, but it is not tested against the philosophical literature that debates what the illative sense actually is. This is the weakest area of the paper's citation coverage relative to the weight the framework bears.

**Key gap:** No secondary Newman scholarship; no engagement with critiques of the illative sense.

### Framework 3: Kahneman's Dual-Process Theory
**Current coverage:** Kahneman (Thinking, Fast and Slow)
**Rating:** WEAK
**Assessment:** A single citation for the entire cognitive architecture of the conscience faculty is insufficient. The paper uses the System 1/System 2 framework as if it were uncontested, but it has been significantly critiqued and revised since 2011. Evans & Stanovich (2013) propose a more nuanced Type 1/Type 2 distinction. Gigerenzer's ecological rationality program argues that "fast" heuristics are often superior to deliberation -- which would complicate the paper's assumption that System 2 escalation always produces better moral judgments. Mercier & Sperber (2017) challenge the assumption that deliberation is truth-seeking. The approximately 30% replication failure rate for classic bias effects (noted in the research) is directly relevant. The paper's claim that tool-call sparsity "solves" the performance objection is computationally interesting but psychologically under-theorized.

**Key gap:** No engagement with any critique or revision of dual-process theory; single citation for an entire framework.

### Framework 4: Ostrom's Commons Governance
**Current coverage:** Ostrom (Governing the Commons)
**Rating:** WEAK-TO-MODERATE
**Assessment:** The paper cites Ostrom's foundational 1990 text and maps all eight design principles onto the conscience faculty. This mapping is well-executed. However, the paper does not cite Ostrom's own later work on knowledge commons (Hess & Ostrom 2007), which is far more relevant to digital AI governance than the fisheries and irrigation cases. The substantial critique literature on applying Ostrom to digital domains -- non-rivalry, scale, anonymity, platform power asymmetries -- is entirely absent. The research sources document serious challenges: over 90% of digital goods are non-rival (undermining excludability assumptions), less than 15% of large-scale open-source projects report effective peer monitoring, and platform power concentration affects governance outcomes. None of these qualifications appears in the paper. The paper also does not engage with Benkler's commons-based peer production or the Governing Knowledge Commons empirical literature.

**Key gap:** No engagement with digital commons critiques; no citation of Ostrom's own digital/knowledge commons work.

## 3. Counter-Arguments from Literature Not Addressed

### 3.1 The Operationalization Gap
The research reveals that less than 10% of AI safety papers explicitly reference virtue ethics, and that the field identifies four major unsolved problems: definition, measurement, implementation, and evaluation of AI virtues (Springer 2025 survey). The paper's conscience faculty proposes an implementation but does not engage with this literature documenting the difficulty of operationalizing virtue concepts.

### 3.2 Relational vs. Individual Moral Agency
Coeckelbergh and others argue that moral agency is constitutively relational -- it emerges from interactions, not from individual faculties. The paper's grounding in Newman's primacy of individual conscience and subsidiarity's allocation of authority to individuals stands in tension with this relational view. The paper acknowledges the anthropomorphism objection (Section 6.1) but does not engage with the deeper relational critique.

### 3.3 Ecological Rationality Against System 2 Supremacy
Gigerenzer's program demonstrates that simple heuristics achieve 80-90% accuracy in ecological decision-making tasks, rivaling complex analytical strategies. This challenges the paper's implicit assumption that System 2 escalation (full moral deliberation) is inherently superior to System 1 heuristic processing. In some domains, the "conscience" may be worse at moral judgment when it deliberates than when it relies on fast heuristics.

### 3.4 Digital Commons Are Not Physical Commons
The Ostrom critique literature identifies fundamental disanalogies between physical and digital commons: (a) digital goods are non-rival and easily replicable, undermining scarcity assumptions; (b) online communities are anonymous, large-scale, and high-turnover, making peer monitoring impractical; (c) platform owners control infrastructure in ways that override community governance. The paper treats the Ostrom analogy as straightforward, but these disanalogies are substantial and unaddressed.

### 3.5 The Society-in-the-Loop Alternative
Rahwan's "society-in-the-loop" framework proposes embedding societal values in AI through collective preference aggregation rather than individual conscience. This represents a genuine alternative that is neither top-down centralized nor bottom-up individualist -- it occupies the middle ground the paper claims does not exist.

### 3.6 Power Concentration in Decentralized Systems
The DAO governance literature documents that despite decentralization aspirations, power routinely concentrates among founders and large token holders. The paper's communal feedback mechanism faces the same risk: early adopters and high-volume users could dominate the "emergent norms" that calibrate new conscience faculties, reproducing centralization under a decentralized label.

### 3.7 Legal Liability Under Principal Sovereignty
If individual operators bear moral responsibility for their agents' actions (as the paper argues), the legal liability framework becomes extremely complex. De Filippi & Wright document that decentralized systems create attribution problems that existing tort and regulatory frameworks cannot handle. The paper's Section 7 mentions "legal analysis of principal sovereignty and liability" as future work, but this is a weakness when the entire architecture depends on principal accountability.

### 3.8 Constitutional AI as Iterative Conscience
The paper cites Amodei (2022) on Constitutional AI but treats it as a centralized approach. However, Constitutional AI can be seen as a form of computationally implemented conscience -- training a model to evaluate its own outputs against principles -- that operates at the model level rather than the tool-call level. The paper should engage with why its architecture is superior to this existing approach rather than simply classifying it as top-down.

## 4. Gap Analysis: Under-Cited Topics

### 4.1 Machine Ethics Literature (Critical Gap)
The paper proposes what is essentially a machine ethics architecture but cites almost none of the machine ethics literature. Wallach, Allen, Moor, Anderson & Anderson, Floridi -- the field's foundational voices are absent. This gives the paper an orphaned quality, as if the conscience faculty were invented without predecessors.

**Recommendation:** Add 3-5 citations from the machine ethics canon, particularly Wallach & Allen (2009) and Moor (2006).

### 4.2 Digital/Knowledge Commons (Significant Gap)
The Ostrom framework carries significant weight in the paper but is supported by a single 1990 citation about physical commons. The knowledge commons literature (Hess & Ostrom 2007; Frischmann et al. 2014; Benkler 2006) is directly relevant and entirely absent. The critique literature on digital commons is also missing.

**Recommendation:** Add Hess & Ostrom (2007) at minimum; engage with at least one critique of Ostrom-for-digital.

### 4.3 Dual-Process Theory Revisions (Significant Gap)
A single 2011 popular-science book supports the entire cognitive architecture. The 2013 Evans & Stanovich revision and Gigerenzer's counter-program are essential for scholarly credibility.

**Recommendation:** Add Evans & Stanovich (2013) and at least one Gigerenzer reference.

### 4.4 AI Governance Scholarship (Moderate Gap)
The paper critiques centralized AI governance but engages only with NIST, the EU AI Act, and AWS/UC Berkeley white papers. The academic AI governance literature (Dafoe 2018, Rahwan 2018, Floridi & Cowls 2019) is absent.

**Recommendation:** Add 2-3 academic AI governance citations to strengthen the critique of centralized approaches by engaging with their strongest versions.

### 4.5 Agentic AI Safety (Moderate Gap)
The paper's practical domain -- agentic AI safety -- is represented only by the Linbeck Guardian Angel preprint, NIST standards, and the AWS/UC Berkeley white papers. The rapidly growing literature on agentic harms (Chan et al. 2023), agentic governance (Shavit et al. 2023), and tool-use safety is not engaged.

**Recommendation:** Add 2-3 recent agentic AI safety papers, particularly from FAccT, NeurIPS, or industry research labs.

### 4.6 Newman Secondary Scholarship (Moderate Gap)
Two primary Newman texts carry enormous argumentative weight without secondary support. The Gage & Aquino (2025) re-examination and Nichols (1985) critique would provide essential scholarly grounding.

**Recommendation:** Add 1-2 secondary Newman sources.

### 4.7 Empirical Validation Literature (Structural Gap)
The paper makes empirical claims about the conscience faculty's performance (sub-100ms System 1, 200-500ms System 2) but cites no empirical evaluation methodology. The agentic AI evaluation literature is growing rapidly and would provide frameworks for validating the paper's claims.

**Recommendation:** Acknowledge evaluation methodology as a gap and cite relevant evaluation frameworks.
